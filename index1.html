AI in ITSM & Ticket Management

How AI is reshaping IT Service Management — and how we are applying it inside our organization.

How we use it

AI-Assisted Ticket Management

In our ITSM workflow, AI helps at every stage of the ticket lifecycle:

  • Smart triage: AI auto-classifies incoming tickets by category, urgency, and impact, and routes them to the right queue.
  • Suggested resolutions: The tool surfaces similar past tickets and KB articles, reducing average handle time.
  • Auto-drafted responses: Copilot-style suggestions help engineers reply consistently and faster.
  • Predictive insights: Trend detection on recurring incidents highlights problem candidates before they escalate.
  • Chatbots for L1: Password resets, software requests, and status lookups are self-served, freeing engineers for complex work.
Impact

Why It Matters

AI in ITSM directly improves the metrics that matter to operations:

  • Faster MTTR through intelligent recommendations.
  • Higher SLA compliance via automatic prioritization.
  • Better user experience with 24/7 self-service.
  • Data-driven capacity planning and problem management.
  • Engineers focus on high-value tasks instead of repetitive requests.
Evolution

The Evolution of AI in IT Operations

1. Rule-Based Automation

Static scripts, macros, and if-then rules automating password resets and basic monitoring alerts.

2. Machine Learning

Pattern recognition for ticket classification, anomaly detection in logs, and predictive incident analytics.

3. Conversational AI

NLP-powered chatbots and virtual agents handling L1 support in Teams, Slack, and service portals.

4. Generative & Agentic AI

LLM-powered copilots that draft responses, summarize incidents, and autonomously execute runbooks with human oversight.

Assessment 1.5 — Reflection on AI/ML Leadership & Change Management

A personal reflection on two self-assessments completed as part of my leadership development.

Strengths

Top 3 Strengths

For the AI/ML Leadership assessment, my strongest areas were technology adaptability, problem-solving, and continuous learning. Working as a Desktop Support Engineer at Tech Mahindra and previously in service desk roles has required me to quickly learn new technologies, troubleshoot complex issues, and stay current with evolving tools such as Azure, Intune, and AI-assisted solutions.

For the Change Management assessment, my top strengths were communication, collaboration, and adaptability. Throughout my career, I have worked with cross-functional teams, supported end users through technology transitions, and helped implement new processes while maintaining service quality.

Growth

Top 3 Areas for Growth

On the AI/ML side, I identified strategic AI planning, AI governance and ethics, and leading large-scale AI initiatives as areas for improvement. While I actively use AI tools and recently worked on the TED Talk Speaker Recommendation Bot project, I would like more experience connecting AI strategies to broader organizational goals.

For Change Management, my areas for growth were managing resistance to change, measuring long-term change success, and influencing stakeholders. Supporting IT changes is a regular part of my role, but leading enterprise-wide change initiatives would help strengthen these skills.

Opinion

My Opinion of the Assessments

I found both assessments useful because they encouraged self-reflection and helped identify practical strengths and development opportunities. Rather than serving as precise measurements, they act as a starting point for leadership growth. One limitation is that self-assessments depend heavily on personal perception, which can sometimes lead to overestimating or underestimating abilities. I believe combining self-assessments with peer or supervisor feedback would provide a more complete evaluation.

Revisions

Revisions I Would Make

For the AI/ML assessment, I would add questions related to ethical AI use, data privacy, cybersecurity considerations, and responsible AI governance. These topics are becoming increasingly important as organizations adopt AI technologies.

For the Change Management assessment, I would include questions that focus on leading remote or hybrid teams, measuring adoption rates, and using data to evaluate the success of change initiatives. Based on the articles and my own experience, I would also add more questions that assess a leader's ability to communicate the "why" behind change, not just the implementation process itself.

Closing Thoughts

Overall, the assessments reinforced the idea that successful change leadership requires a balance of technical knowledge, communication skills, adaptability, and a commitment to continuous learning. As AI continues to evolve and become more integrated into business operations, leaders must be able to guide both technology adoption and the people impacted by that change.

Matching the Tool to the Problem: Machine Learning vs. Deep Learning

A case study comparing two real-world AI applications to show when traditional machine learning is the right choice, when deep learning earns its cost — and why picking the most advanced tool is not always the right business decision.

Objective

Evaluate two real-world AI applications to determine when traditional ML versus deep learning is the appropriate approach, and communicate that decision framework to a business audience.

Process

Selected two contrasting applications — customer churn prediction and CNN-based facial recognition — and analyzed each against three criteria: feature extraction, data requirements, and model complexity.

Tools & Concepts

Support vector machines, logistic regression, convolutional neural networks; evaluation framing drawn from my analytics work in Python, SQL, and Power BI.

Value Proposition

Demonstrates the ability to match an AI approach to business constraints — interpretability, data availability, and cost — a judgment skill central to AI adoption decisions in IT operations.

Traditional ML — Customer Churn

Structured tabular data, known features, modest datasets, and a business need for interpretable predictions.

VS

Deep Learning — Image Recognition

Raw, high-dimensional pixel data where features can't be hand-defined and massive datasets justify network complexity.

Machine learning and deep learning both enable computers to learn from data, but they differ fundamentally in how they handle features, how much data they require, and how complex their models are. A simple way to frame the difference: machine learning is like giving a student a checklist of things to look for, while deep learning is like teaching the student to read and letting them figure out the patterns through experience. This case study compares one real-world application of each approach and explains why the chosen technique fits the problem while the alternative does not.

Machine Learning: Customer Churn Prediction

Telecommunications and subscription-based companies use models such as support vector machines and logistic regression to predict which customers are likely to cancel. A provider can analyze historical records — contract length, monthly charges, tenure, support tickets filed — to flag at-risk customers before they leave, allowing retention teams to intervene with targeted offers.

Traditional ML fits this problem for three reasons. First, the data is structured and tabular, and the meaningful features are already known to domain experts, so manual feature selection is straightforward and effective. Second, churn datasets are often modest in size — enough for an SVM or logistic regression to perform well. Third, the business needs interpretability: a retention team must understand why a customer was flagged, and simpler models provide that transparency directly.

Deep learning is not suitable here. Its core advantage — automatically discovering features from raw, high-dimensional data — is wasted on a few dozen clean database columns. A deep neural network would risk overfitting the limited data, demand far more computational power, and produce a black-box model that is harder to explain to stakeholders, typically with no accuracy improvement over traditional methods on tabular data.

Deep Learning: Image Recognition with CNNs

Facial recognition systems such as Apple's Face ID use convolutional neural networks to identify users from raw camera data. The CNN processes millions of pixel values and learns, through its many layers, to detect progressively more abstract patterns — from simple edges to textures to complete facial structures — without any human defining what an eye or nose looks like.

Deep learning fits this problem precisely because the input is raw and unstructured. No human can manually write features that reliably describe a face across every lighting condition, angle, and individual. The automatic, layered feature learning of a deep network is exactly what the task demands, and today's enormous image datasets provide the training volume that deep learning requires.

Traditional ML is not suitable for this task. An SVM or decision tree fed raw pixels has no mechanism for discovering spatial patterns on its own, so it would depend entirely on hand-engineered image features — an approach that historically capped accuracy well below human performance. The complexity of a deep network is justified here because the problem itself is complex.

Conclusion

When features are known, data is limited, and interpretability matters, traditional machine learning is the better tool. When the data is raw and high-dimensional, features cannot be defined by hand, and large datasets are available, deep learning's added complexity earns its cost. Choosing between them is less about which is more advanced and more about matching the tool to the shape of the problem.
